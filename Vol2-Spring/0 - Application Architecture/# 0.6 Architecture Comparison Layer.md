# 0.6 Architecture Comparison: Layered vs Clean vs Hexagonal

By now, we've explored three popular architectural styles:

- Layered Architecture
- Clean Architecture
- Hexagonal Architecture

A common question among developers is:

> **"Which one should I use?"**

The answer is:

> **It depends.**

There is no universally superior architecture.

Each solves different problems and introduces different trade-offs.

A Senior Backend Engineer is expected to choose the architecture that best fits the business, team, and operational context.

---

# Evolution of Enterprise Applications

Many successful systems evolve through architectural stages rather than starting with the most sophisticated design.

```text
Simple Monolith

        ↓

Layered Architecture

        ↓

Modular Monolith

        ↓

Clean / Hexagonal Architecture

        ↓

Microservices (when justified)
```

Notice that architecture often evolves incrementally as complexity increases.

---

# High-Level Comparison

| Aspect | Layered | Clean | Hexagonal |
|---------|----------|--------|------------|
| Primary Goal | Separation of responsibilities | Business independence | Technology independence |
| Learning Curve | Low | Medium | Medium |
| Complexity | Low | Medium | Medium–High |
| Testability | Good | Excellent | Excellent |
| Infrastructure Coupling | Medium | Low | Low |
| Suitable for CRUD Apps | Excellent | Good | Fair |
| Suitable for Complex Domains | Good | Excellent | Excellent |
| Framework Independence | Limited | High | High |
| Database Independence | Moderate | High | High |
| External Integrations | Moderate | Good | Excellent |

# Core Philosophy

## Layered Architecture

Organize code into logical layers.

```text
Controller

↓

Service

↓

Repository
```

The focus is on **responsibility separation**.

---

## Clean Architecture

Keep business rules independent of frameworks.

```text
Framework

↓

Application

↓

Domain
```

The focus is on **dependency direction**.

---

## Hexagonal Architecture

Keep business logic independent of external systems.

```text
Application

↓

Ports

↓

Adapters
```

The focus is on **communication boundaries**.

---

# Dependency Direction

### Layered Architecture

```text
Controller

↓

Service

↓

Repository
```

Concrete implementations are commonly referenced directly.

---

### Clean Architecture

```text
Infrastructure

↓

Application

↓

Domain
```

Dependencies point inward.

---

### Hexagonal Architecture

```text
REST

↓

Input Port

↓

Use Case

↓

Output Port

↓

Database Adapter
```

Business logic communicates only through ports.

Simple and familiar.

## Clean

```text

```


# Testing Comparison

## Layered

Business logic often requires mocking repositories.

Integration with Spring is common.

---

## Clean

Business logic can usually be tested with plain unit tests.

No Spring Context required.

---

## Hexagonal

Use cases can be tested using mock implementations of ports.

Infrastructure can be tested independently.

---

# Scalability

Layered Architecture scales well for:

- Small systems
- Medium systems
- Traditional enterprise applications

---

Clean Architecture scales well for:

- Large business domains
- Long-lived systems
- Teams focused on maintainability

---

Hexagonal Architecture scales well for:

- Systems with many integrations
- Event-driven architectures
- Payment systems
- Financial platforms
- Enterprise SaaS

---

# Team Size

### Small Team (2–5 Developers)

Layered Architecture is often sufficient.

The communication overhead of more complex architectures may not provide enough benefit.

---

### Medium Team (5–20 Developers)

Layered or Modular Monolith with selected Clean Architecture principles works well.

Clear module ownership becomes increasingly important.

---

### Large Organization (20+ Developers)

Clean and Hexagonal approaches help teams maintain boundaries and reduce unintended coupling.

# When to Choose Layered Architecture

Choose Layered Architecture when:

- Building CRUD-heavy applications
- Developing internal business tools
- Team members are familiar with Spring Boot conventions
- Time-to-market is a priority
- Infrastructure is relatively stable

Example:

```text
Employee Management System
```

Simple.

Easy to understand.

Easy to maintain.

---

# When to Choose Clean Architecture

Choose Clean Architecture when:

- Business rules are complex
- Long-term maintainability matters
- Infrastructure may change over time
- Extensive unit testing is desired
- Domain logic should remain framework-independent

Example:

```text
Loan Approval Engine
```

The business logic deserves protection from infrastructure concerns.

---

# When to Choose Hexagonal Architecture

Choose Hexagonal Architecture when:

- The application integrates with many external systems
- Multiple communication channels exist
- Technology providers may change
- The domain is integration-heavy

Example:

```text
Payment Gateway
```

Supporting multiple payment providers becomes much easier.

---

# Common Misconceptions

## "Hexagonal Replaces Clean"

No.

They solve different concerns.

Many systems combine both.

---

## "Clean Is Better Than Layered"

Not always.

If the problem is simple, Layered Architecture may be the better engineering choice.

Architecture should solve problems, not create them.

---

## "Every Project Needs Ports"

No.

Ports introduce abstraction.

Abstraction should be introduced only where flexibility is valuable.

---

## "Interfaces Improve Design"

Not automatically.

An interface without multiple implementations or a meaningful boundary often adds unnecessary complexity.

# Real-World Evolution

A startup often begins with:

```text
Spring Boot

↓

Controller

↓

Service

↓

Repository
```

As the product grows:

```text
Modules
```

are introduced.

Later:

```text
Ports

Adapters

Use Cases
```

may be added around complex business areas.

Finally:

Selected modules become:

```text
Microservices
```

Very few successful systems begin with dozens of microservices and a fully implemented Hexagonal Architecture.

Architecture typically evolves alongside the business.

---

# What Do Large Companies Actually Use?

One of the biggest misconceptions is that companies choose **one architecture**.

In reality, they combine patterns.

A typical service inside a large enterprise may look like:

```text
Microservice

↓

Layered Package Structure

↓

Clean Architecture Dependency Rules

↓

Hexagonal Ports for External Systems
```

This hybrid approach provides:

- Familiar organization
- Strong business isolation
- Flexible integrations

without forcing every component into the same pattern.

---

At the same time:

The service is:

- Clean in dependency direction
- Hexagonal at integration boundaries

This is a common enterprise design.

# Migration Strategy

Avoid large architectural rewrites.

Instead, evolve gradually.

Example:

## Phase 1

```text
Controller

↓

Service

↓

Repository
```

---

## Phase 2

Extract business rules.

```text
Service

↓

Use Cases
```

---

## Phase 3

Introduce ports around infrastructure.

```text
Use Case

↓

Repository Port

↓

JPA Adapter
```

---

## Phase 4

Split mature modules into independent services if business and organizational needs justify it.

Incremental evolution is generally less risky than large-scale rewrites.

# Interview Questions

### Q1. Which architecture would you recommend for a new Spring Boot project?

Start with the simplest architecture that satisfies the current requirements. For most projects, a well-structured Layered Architecture or Modular Monolith is an excellent starting point. Introduce additional architectural patterns only when complexity justifies them.

---

### Q2. Can Layered Architecture evolve into Hexagonal Architecture?

Yes.

By introducing ports around infrastructure concerns and extracting business use cases, a layered application can gradually adopt Hexagonal principles without a complete rewrite.

---

### Q3. Is Clean Architecture framework-independent?

Yes.

Its goal is to keep business logic independent of frameworks, allowing technologies to change without affecting core business rules.

---

### Q4. Is Hexagonal Architecture mainly about testing?

Testing is an important benefit, but the primary goal is isolating the application from external systems through ports and adapters.

---

### Q5. Which architecture do large companies use?

Most large organizations use a combination of architectural patterns rather than a single "pure" architecture. The choice depends on the complexity of the domain, integration requirements, and team structure.

---

# Senior Interview Perspective

Suppose an interviewer asks:

> **"Which architecture would you use for a payment processing platform?"**

A balanced answer would be:

> "I would organize the service internally using a layered structure for readability, apply Clean Architecture principles to keep business rules independent, and introduce Hexagonal Ports & Adapters around external integrations such as payment providers, databases, Kafka, and notifications. This provides maintainability while allowing infrastructure to evolve independently."

This demonstrates practical engineering judgment instead of advocating a single architecture for every problem.

---

# Key Takeaways

- No architecture is universally best; each addresses different challenges.
- Layered Architecture emphasizes separation of responsibilities.
- Clean Architecture emphasizes dependency direction and protecting business rules.
- Hexagonal Architecture emphasizes isolating the application from external systems through ports and adapters.
- Enterprise systems commonly combine these patterns rather than following one in isolation.
- Architecture should evolve gradually based on business needs, technical complexity, and organizational growth rather than adopting sophisticated patterns prematurely.
